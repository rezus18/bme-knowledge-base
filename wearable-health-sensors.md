# Wearable Health Sensors

## Overview
Wearable health sensors are devices worn on the body that continuously monitor physiological parameters. They are widely used in clinical monitoring, sports performance, and consumer health applications.

## Types of Wearable Sensors

### 1. Heart Rate Monitors
- Use **photoplethysmography (PPG)** — measure blood volume changes via light
- Common in smartwatches (Apple Watch, Fitbit, Garmin)
- Can detect arrhythmias (e.g., atrial fibrillation)

### 2. Accelerometers & Gyroscopes
- Measure movement and orientation
- Used for step counting, fall detection, sleep analysis
- Often combined as IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit)

### 3. Skin Temperature Sensors
- Monitor body temperature trends
- Useful for detecting fever, ovulation tracking, stress response

### 4. Electrodermal Activity (EDA) Sensors
- Measure skin conductance (sweat gland activity)
- Indicator of emotional arousal and stress

### 5. Blood Oxygen Sensors (SpO2)
- Use two wavelengths of light (red and infrared)
- Important for monitoring respiratory conditions (e.g., sleep apnea)

## Key Challenges
- **Power consumption** — battery life vs. continuous monitoring
- **Motion artifacts** — movement can distort PPG and ECG signals
- **Data security** — sensitive health data transmission via Bluetooth
- **Accuracy** — consumer devices vs. medical-grade equipment

## Applications
- Remote patient monitoring
- Chronic disease management (diabetes, heart disease)
- Sports and fitness tracking
- Early warning systems for deterioration

## Standards & Connectivity
- Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) — most common protocol
- ANT+ — used in sports devices
- IEEE 11073 — personal health device communication standard
